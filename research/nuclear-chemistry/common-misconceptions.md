# Nuclear Chemistry — Common Misconceptions

## Misconception NUC-MC001

Misconception: Nuclide and isotope are interchangeable labels for any atom.

Correct explanation: A nuclide is a specified nuclear species. Isotopes are nuclides of the same element with the same atomic number and different mass numbers.

Evidence: NUC-002; NUC-003

Verification: Verified

Educational notes: The nuclide-chart visual must keep element identity on proton number and distinguish mass number from neutron number.

## Misconception NUC-MC002

Misconception: One half-life is the time at which every nucleus in a sample has decayed.

Correct explanation: Half-life describes the decrease of activity for an ensemble under one decay process. The decay constant is a probability-per-time quantity; an individual decay time is not set by the half-life equation.

Evidence: NUC-006; NUC-007; NUC-012

Verification: Verified

Educational notes: Use an ensemble visual and never animate all nuclei disappearing at one threshold time.

## Misconception NUC-MC003

Misconception: Detector count rate equals source activity.

Correct explanation: Activity is a decay rate. A measured count rate is conditioned by geometry, detector efficiency, emission probability, background, live time, attenuation, and other corrections.

Evidence: NUC-005; NUC-014

Verification: Verified

Educational notes: Put the physical source boundary before the instrument-response and analysis boundaries.

## Misconception NUC-MC004

Misconception: One shielding material or thickness is appropriate for every radiation source.

Correct explanation: Source form, radiation type and energy, activity, geometry, exposure pathway, and operational purpose determine the protection design. A sealed-source device can collimate useful radiation while shielding personnel from unwanted exposure.

Evidence: NUC-018

Verification: Verified

Educational notes: Do not present lead as a universal shield or show shielding without a named radiation and geometry.

## Misconception NUC-MC005

Misconception: Radioisotope production ends when a target is irradiated.

Correct explanation: Reactor or accelerator production is followed by chemical processing into the required form, and medical products require a controlled quality system before use.

Evidence: NUC-015; NUC-016

Verification: Verified

Educational notes: Separate nuclear production, radiochemical separation or processing, product quality control, authorized use, and end-of-life management.

## Misconception NUC-MC006

Misconception: Storage and disposal are interchangeable end states for a radioactive source.

Correct explanation: Storage preserves control pending a later decision or operation. Disposal is the final lifecycle phase, and the applicable route depends on radionuclide, activity, source form, waste acceptance criteria, and regulation.

Evidence: NUC-017

Verification: Verified

Educational notes: The lifecycle flow must show storage as a managed state and disposal as a separate terminal route.
